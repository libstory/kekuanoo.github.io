Overview

Podcasts often follow a similar production arc but can vary greatly based on the audience and genre of their narrative. In all likelihood, this is a podcast you are putting together for the first time. However, you have already started to build a strong foundation to develop your podcast. You have created a plan that includes a Big Idea that you want to explore, identified an audience that you want to reach, envisioned some outcomes for your production and how you want to connect with your audience, and reflected on the work that you intend to develop.

By clarifying all of these steps, you have started to make considerations for your work that will hopefully meet your desired intent and impact. Now, it's time to identify the stages of a podcast production and how it impacts various genres of the narrative form.

Please note that these stages are meant to be iterative and non-linear. You may flow freely from stage to stage, skip a stage, return to a previous stage, and so on.


Stage 1: Where do I begin?

Come up with an idea and make a plan. Consider the following for each genre type:

Interviews

  • Who do you want to interview?
  • Why do you want to interview them?
  • What do you want to talk about?
  • Will this be a peaceful interview? Or a heated one?

Investigative Reporting

  • What is your subject of investigation?
  • What closed questions do you have about it? (Questions that have definitive answers.)
  • What open questions do you have about it? (Questions that don’t have definitive answers.)
  • Where do you have to go to investigate it?
  • Who do you need to talk to?

Panel Discussion & Team Comedy

  • Who should be on the panel?
  • What is the panel's theme?
  • What does the panel speak about?
  • Where are the boundaries?
  • Does the panel script or improvise your discussion?
  • Does the panel challenge themes or genres?

Solo Commentary, Comedy & Fiction

  • What story do you want to tell?
  • What questions haunt you, plague you, or enchant you about this story?
  • Which arguments could you sustain for an audience?
  • What weird ideas do you have?
  • What is the set up for your story?
  • What is a story, anyway?


Stage 2: Conducting the recording.

Do some research before you record so you can generate interesting and intriguing questions to explore with your subject or characters. In the podcast industry, this is called your “Q Line.” You can share these questions with your subject before the recording, but don't feel obligated to do so if you want a more natural reaction.

Set a time and place for the recording. Before you record, make sure to confirm good sound levels in your environment. Don’t be afraid to throw out your Q Line if it’s not working or if you organically come across better conversation. This process of recording is in an effort to generate another podcast industry term called “Tape.” Tape is basically the recording assets you collect during the production process.

As you finish recording your subjects, consider sharing a consent or release form that they can sign.

Interviews

    • Is the interview in a place where you can control the sound? If so, prep.
    • Or is it in a place that you’re unfamiliar with? If so, more prep.
    • Is the interview on the phone? If so, Google Voice.

Investigative Reporting

  • Are you recording in the field? Scout your environment.
  • Are there environmental noises or cross-talk to consider?
  • Is the interview on the phone? If so, Google Voice.


Panel Discussion & Team Comedy

  • Does your microphone pick up everyone's voice? Test it.
  • Is everyone on board and consenting to the topic?
  • Has everyone completed the pre-discussion research necessary to contribute?

Solo Commentary, Comedy & Fiction

  • Have you tested the sound in your recording environment? Record and listen to a small snippet.
  • Do you have a script or outline to follow? Write it and review it.
  • Have you practiced reading the script? Rehearse before recording.

Stage 3: Selecting podcast assets.

Transcribe your Tape using a transcription tool or service. O Transcribe is one example of a tool that works well. Once you're done transcribing, consult your transcription to pick your “Acts.” Acts, or actualities, are the interesting parts of the recording that you’ll use in your podcast. Consider these recorded snippets as "soundbites" from your recording.

You also need to write your Tracks.” Tracks are the scripted, narrated sections that help tie together your Acts. Record your Tracks.

At this point, you should also determine if you need music and sound design including creating, licensing, or finding Creative Commons music and sounds.

Interviews

  • Consider saving each act, or soundbite, as separate recording files to easily import them during your production.

Investigative Reporting

  • Your acts are likely to be saved as separate sound files through reporting. Decide on an appropriate naming convention to quickly and easily locate these recorded snippets.


Panel Discussion & Team Comedy

  • Consider how you might develop an intro or outro to open or close your show.

Solo Commentary, Comedy & Fiction

  • Consider how you might break up your recording sessions so that snippets all have a similar sound and feel. A session recorded in the morning when your vocal chords are still warming up will sound different than later in the afternoon.
  • Also consider how environmental sounds might change between recording sessions.


Stage 4: Edit in Audacity.

Put everything together with Audacity--your Acts, Tracks, music, and sounds. Share the draft of your podcast with any collaborators to get feedback. Share it with workshop cohort members in Slack. Consider, reject, or incorporate feedback. Reflect on your process and the meaning of your podcast. Repeat previous steps.

This is equally applicable to all genre formats.


Stage 5: Finish the transcription, metadata and audio deliverables.

Using the metadata spreadsheet in our workshop materials (participants-only), enter the metadata for all files and assets you created or used. Your complete transcript is the one you will provide for you audience. It will be a combination of your Acts, Tracks, and descriptions of any of the music and sounds you used. Decide, as a whole, whether or not your podcast should have a Creative Commons designation.

This is equally applicable to all genre formats.


Step 6: Determine your own license and consider your digital safety.

Decide, as a whole, whether or not your podcast should have a Creative Commons designation. If you are going to put your podcasts online, do an audit of your online safety and security. Do you have safe passwords? Consider making passwords using the “diceware” method and using LastPass. If you Google yourself, what information do data brokers have about you? Consider contacting data brokers and requesting they remove your information.

But is this the only way to make an interview podcast?

No! The steps above are a guide--not the rule. You might do things in a different order. For example, you might write some of your Acts before conducting your interview and writing your transcript. Or, early on, you might find music that you want to use before you even decide who you want to interview. You might find that some of these steps don’t even apply. For instance, journalists don’t always have interview subjects sign release forms because they operate under the assumption that their subjects have agreed to be on the record.

Have you thought about...?
  • Accessibility: Can all users use your content? Think about users who may want to listen to your content but who are hard of hearing or deaf. Provide a transcript so they can consume what you made.
  • Preservation: You've put time and effort into your team production. Think about filling out your metadata spreadsheet and where and how you will archive your podcast (multiple locations = good!).
  • Open: Have you provided attribution for works other than your own? Think about how you are incorporating content and how you intend others to use your work in the future.
  • Digital Identity, Safety & Privacy: Did you mention someone in your recording by name? Think about how what you do and say can be linked back to you or someone else's identity.
